Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is one of the most researched approaches in addiction treatment, but the actual sessions look less like willpower-building and more like pattern-mapping.

## The core idea

CBT works from a simple premise: thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are linked, and substance use is often a learned response to specific triggers — stress, certain people, certain places, certain emotional states. The work is identifying that chain, and building a different response into it.

## What a typical focus looks like

- Mapping personal triggers and high-risk situations
- Identifying the automatic thoughts that show up right before use ("I've already had a bad day, might as well")
- Building and rehearsing alternative responses before they're needed, not in the moment
- Addressing the beliefs underneath the pattern, not just the behavior itself

## Why this matters beyond willpower

Recovery framed purely as willpower tends to set people up to blame themselves after a setback. CBT reframes setbacks as information about which triggers or thoughts still need a stronger plan — which tends to be a more sustainable way to keep going.
