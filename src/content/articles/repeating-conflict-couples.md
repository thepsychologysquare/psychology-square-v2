Most couples who come into therapy for "the same fight" aren't actually fighting about the dishes, or being late, or the in-laws. Those are the surface topic — the actual pattern underneath tends to repeat regardless of what the argument is nominally about.

## The topic isn't the pattern

If a couple argues about chores one week and travel plans the next, but both arguments end the same way — one person shutting down, the other escalating to be heard — the real issue is that cycle, not either topic.

## Common underlying patterns

- **Pursue–withdraw:** one partner pushes for connection or resolution, the other retreats, which increases the pursuit, which increases the retreat.
- **Escalation:** both partners raise intensity to be heard, and the original issue gets lost under the tone of the fight itself.
- **Stonewalling:** one partner disengages entirely as a way of self-protecting, which often reads to the other as not caring.

## Why naming the pattern helps

Once a couple can recognize "we're doing the pursue–withdraw thing again" in the moment, it becomes something they're both looking at together, rather than something happening to one of them. That shift — from opponents to a shared problem — is usually where real change starts.
